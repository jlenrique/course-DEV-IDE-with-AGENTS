# Narration Script: Core Characteristics of Innovative Mindset

## Header

- **Lesson Plan Reference:** LP-C1M1L1
- **Script ID:** NS-C1M1L1-02
- **Paired Slide Brief:** SB-C1M1L1-02
- **Learning Objective:** LO1 — Define key characteristics of an innovative mindset
- **Bloom's Level:** Remember

## Script Body

### Segment 1

**[Slide Reference: SB-C1M1L1-02a]**

**Stage Directions:**
- Tone: Professional, clear, grounding
- Pacing: Measured — give each definition time to land
- Emphasis: *flexibility*, *resiliency*, *adaptability*, *ambiguity*

**Narration:**
Let's define what we mean by an *innovative mindset*. It's built on four core characteristics that you already use in clinical practice — you just may not have named them in the context of innovation.

**Transition:**
Let's take each one in turn, starting with flexibility.

### Segment 2

**[Slide Reference: SB-C1M1L1-02b]**

**Stage Directions:**
- Tone: Conversational, relatable
- Pacing: Moderate with brief pauses between definitions
- Emphasis: *change approach*, *not abandoning standards*

**Narration:**
*Flexibility* means willingness to change your approach when evidence suggests a different path. In clinical practice, you do this constantly — adjusting a treatment plan when labs come back unexpected. In innovation, it means pivoting a project direction when user feedback contradicts your assumptions. It's not about abandoning standards — it's about holding your method loosely while holding your goal tightly.

*Resiliency* is your capacity to recover from setbacks without losing momentum. Every physician knows this from training — the cases that didn't go as planned, the research that didn't replicate. In innovation, setbacks aren't failures. They're data points that narrow the path to a working solution.

**Transition:**
The next two characteristics build on these.

### Segment 3

**[Slide Reference: SB-C1M1L1-02c]**

**Stage Directions:**
- Tone: Slightly more challenging, provocative
- Pacing: Deliberate — the ambiguity concept requires processing time
- Emphasis: *incomplete information*, *comfortable with not knowing*

**Narration:**
*Adaptability* is adjusting to new conditions in real time. Clinical environments change hour by hour. Innovation environments change market by market. The skill transfers directly — you're already adaptable, you just need to apply it to a different domain.

And then there's *comfort with ambiguity*. This is the hardest one for physicians, because medical training emphasizes certainty — right diagnosis, right treatment, right outcome. Innovation requires you to operate with incomplete information and be comfortable with not knowing the answer yet. The good news? You already do this in clinical uncertainty. A differential diagnosis is essentially a list of hypotheses you're comfortable holding simultaneously until evidence resolves them. Innovation thinking works the same way.
