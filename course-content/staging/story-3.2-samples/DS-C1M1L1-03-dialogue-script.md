# Dialogue Script: Working with Ambiguity — Clinical Parallel

## Header

- **Lesson Plan Reference:** LP-C1M1L1
- **Script ID:** DS-C1M1L1-03
- **Learning Objective:** LO2 — Explain how comfort with ambiguity contributes to innovation
- **Bloom's Level:** Understand
- **Scenario Type:** Clinical case paralleling innovation decision-making

## Characters

| Character | Role | Voice Profile | Tone |
|-----------|------|--------------|------|
| Dr. Reyes | Attending physician / innovation lead | Calm, experienced, reflective | Confident but open |
| Dr. Kim | Resident / early-career innovator | Curious, slightly uncertain | Eager but cautious |

## Dialogue Body

**[SCENE: Hospital conference room. Dr. Reyes and Dr. Kim are reviewing market data for a patient-engagement app Dr. Kim is developing.]**

**Dr. Kim:** I've been looking at the usage data from our pilot, and honestly, I'm not sure what to make of it. Patients over 65 are using the app more than we expected, but the 30-to-45 group — our target — is barely engaging.

*[Stage direction: Genuine uncertainty, not distress]*

**Dr. Reyes:** What's your instinct telling you?

**Dr. Kim:** That we built it for the wrong audience. But we don't have enough data to be sure. It's only been three weeks.

**[PEDAGOGICAL BEAT: The learner should recognize this as clinical reasoning under uncertainty — insufficient data, multiple possible explanations, pressure to act.]**

**Dr. Reyes:** Sound familiar? How many times in the ED have you had a patient with ambiguous labs and an unclear history, and you had to decide whether to admit or discharge?

**Dr. Kim:** Every shift. But that's different — I have clinical frameworks for that. Differential diagnosis, pre-test probability...

**Dr. Reyes:** And what are those frameworks, really? They're structured ways to operate with incomplete information. Innovation has the same thing. Your pilot data is your labs. Your user interviews are your patient history. You don't need certainty — you need a structured way to narrow the possibilities.

*[Stage direction: Moment of recognition]*

**Dr. Kim:** So instead of deciding whether we built it wrong, I should be asking — what additional data would narrow my differential?

**[PEDAGOGICAL BEAT: The learner should connect the clinical framework (differential diagnosis) to the innovation framework (hypothesis-driven iteration).]**

**Dr. Reyes:** Exactly. Run another two weeks with targeted user interviews for the 30-to-45 cohort. That's your next test. You're not failing — you're iterating.

**Dr. Kim:** That... actually makes sense. I've been treating this like it needs to work perfectly the first time.

**Dr. Reyes:** That's the fixed-mindset trap. In clinical training, we sometimes teach that there's always a right answer. In innovation, the right answer emerges from the process. Your comfort with ambiguity *is* the skill.
